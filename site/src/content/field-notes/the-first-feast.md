---
title: The First Feast
date: "2024-09-21"
location: "Minneapolis, MN"
category: gathering
excerpt: Twelve people, one folding table, a borrowed park permit. What happened when a neighborhood decided to eat together.
---

The permit cost fourteen dollars and took three weeks to arrive. Nobody had a table long enough, so they borrowed two folding ones from the church on Nicollet and carried them four blocks on a Saturday morning.

Twelve people came to the first one. A retired teacher brought a pot of black bean soup. A young couple from the building on the corner made injera. Someone's grandmother arrived with a tin of cookies she'd been baking since 6 a.m. The folding tables were too short for twelve chairs end to end, so they improvised with lawn chairs, a camping stool, and a garden bench someone dragged from their backyard.

It lasted three hours. Nobody planned three hours. It just kept going.

---

Mara had moved to the neighborhood eight months earlier and knew three people by name. She had the idea after walking past Powderhorn Park one evening and thinking about how strange it was to live surrounded by hundreds of people you had never spoken to. She mentioned it at a block association meeting — just a stray thought — and two people said yes immediately and one more said she'd help if she didn't have to be in charge.

That became the model. Nobody in charge. Everybody responsible for something.

---

The second month, nineteen people came. The third month, thirty-one. By the following spring, someone had donated a proper folding table — the long kind, the kind you see at community centers — and they stopped needing to improvise.

But the improvised quality of the first one is what people remember. The camping stool. The soup that ran out before everyone got some and the way that became a conversation instead of an embarrassment. The cookies.

"We were just people in a park," Mara said, two years later. "But something shifted. I don't know how to explain it except that after that I started to know where I lived."

---

The Minneapolis Great Feast now gathers monthly, April through October, in Powderhorn Park. Attendance ranges from 40 to 150 depending on weather and season. Seasonal menus are anchored by vegetables from two local farms. Musicians rotate through — mostly neighbors who play for the pleasure of it.

Nobody is in charge. Everybody is responsible for something.

The permit now costs eighteen dollars.
