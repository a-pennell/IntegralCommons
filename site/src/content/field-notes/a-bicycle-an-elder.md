He was seventy-three and had been fixing bicycles since before most of the volunteers were born. He didn't talk much at first — just watched people work, offered corrections in short sentences, handed tools without being asked.

His name was Ernie. He had retired from thirty years at a machine shop in North Portland and found, in retirement, that he had no idea what to do with his hands.

---

The Portland Repair Commons meets on the second Saturday of every month in a church parking lot on Alberta Street. The format is simple: bring something broken, leave with it fixed or with a clearer understanding of why it can't be. Volunteers with specific skills — bicycle mechanics, electronics repair, textile mending, knife sharpening — set up stations. Everyone else circulates.

Kyra was seventeen. She had a ten-speed she'd found in a dumpster and couldn't figure out why the gears wouldn't shift. She had watched three YouTube videos and given up.

Ernie looked at the derailleur for about four seconds. "Cable tension," he said. "You want to learn or you want me to fix it?"

She said she wanted to learn.

---

Two hours later, the bike worked. More importantly, Kyra understood why it worked — not the theory, but the feel of it, the specific resistance of a cable correctly tensioned, the sound the chain made when it dropped cleanly into the right gear. Knowledge that had lived in Ernie's hands for fifty years was now, partially, in hers.

This is what repair gatherings actually do. Not primarily divert waste from landfills (though they do that too). Not primarily save money (though that). They are places where knowledge that would otherwise disappear — knowledge held in the bodies of people who learned from people who are gone — gets passed sideways and forward.

Ernie came back the next month. And the month after that. He started showing up early to help set up the tables.

---

The Portland Repair Commons has repaired more than 1,400 items since it started three years ago. They estimate roughly 3,200 pounds of material diverted from the waste stream. They have stopped tracking the number of mentorships that have formed, because it got too hard to define when a two-hour interaction becomes something more.

Kyra's bike, last anyone checked, still runs fine.
